TOPIC: Mutexes and Locks - Thread Synchronization

Protecting shared data from race conditions

What this carousel covers:
→ std::mutex fundamentals and race condition prevention mechanisms
→ RAII lock wrappers (lock_guard, unique_lock, scoped_lock) for exception-safe locking
→ Mutex types: recursive_mutex, timed_mutex, shared_mutex for readers-writer scenarios
→ Deadlock prevention with std::lock() and consistent lock ordering strategies
→ Lock granularity trade-offs: coarse-grained vs fine-grained locking patterns
→ Performance characteristics: uncontended locks (~10-30ns) vs contended (~1-10μs)
→ Deferred locking, std::adopt_lock, and std::defer_lock usage patterns
→ Real-world patterns: thread-safe singletons, container protection, multi-mutex locking

Key insights:
◆ std::lock_guard provides automatic RAII unlock - exception-safe and prevents lock leaks even during stack unwinding
◆ std::unique_lock offers flexibility: manual lock/unlock, deferred locking, required for condition variables
◆ std::shared_mutex enables multiple concurrent readers or one exclusive writer (C++17 readers-writer lock)
◆ Lock ordering deadlock: Thread A locks m1→m2, Thread B locks m2→m1 creates circular wait - use std::lock() to prevent
◆ std::adopt_lock tells lock wrappers "mutex already locked, just manage lifetime" - critical after std::lock()
◆ Recursive mutexes allow same thread to relock (tracks ownership + count) but have 1.5-2x overhead
◆ Minimize lock hold time: avoid I/O, unknown callbacks, or slow operations in critical sections to reduce contention
◆ Futex-based mutexes are fast when uncontended (~10-30ns atomic ops) but expensive under contention (~1-10μs syscalls)
